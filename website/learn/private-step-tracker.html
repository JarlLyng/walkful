How to choose a private step tracker

Buyer's guide · ~4 min read

Your daily movement is sensitive data. Here's how to find a step tracker that treats it that way — and what the warning signs look like.

"Free" fitness apps aren't always free. Many fund themselves by collecting your activity and selling it to advertisers or data brokers, or by parking it on their servers behind an account you didn't really need. Step data can reveal where you live, when you sleep, your routines and your health — so it's worth a moment to choose carefully.

Quick checklist: look for on-device processing, no required account, an App Store "Data Not Collected" label, sensible permissions, and a one-time price rather than a subscription that incentivizes hoarding your data.

1. Where is your data processed?

The single biggest factor is whether the app does its work on your device or sends your data to a server. On-device apps can read your steps from Apple Health, do all the calculations locally, and never transmit anything. No server means there's simply nothing to leak, subpoena, or sell.

2. Does it require an account?

A login almost always means your data lives on someone else's computer. For a step tracker, you rarely need one. If an app demands an email and password just to count your steps, ask what it's doing with them.

3. Check the App Store privacy label

Every App Store listing has an "App Privacy" section. The strongest result is "Data Not Collected" — the developer declares the app collects nothing at all. "Data Used to Track You" is the opposite signal. It takes ten seconds to check before you install.

4. Look at the permissions it asks for

A step tracker needs read access to your activity data. It does not need your contacts, your precise location in the background, or the ability to write to your health record. Over-broad permission requests are a red flag.

5. Mind the business model

How an app makes money shapes how it treats you. Ads and "free" tiers are often paid for with your data. A one-time purchase aligns the app with you rather than with advertisers, and removes the incentive to harvest behavior to keep you subscribed.
